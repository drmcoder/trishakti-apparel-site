## PART 2 — Rules of origin: why your model works, and the clock on it

**Why Chinese fabric is fine (your biggest asset, not a liability):**
Under Nepal's LDC status, the EU applies the **"single transformation" rule of origin** to
garments. Because the garment is **cut and sewn in Nepal**, it qualifies as **Nepali origin
regardless of where the fabric came from.** So:

> Chinese fabric + sewn in Nepal = "Made in Nepal" = **0% duty into the EU** (vs. the 12% MFN
> rate a non-preferential origin pays).

**The clock — this is the single most important strategic fact:**

| Phase | Dates | Your duty status | Rule of origin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Now (LDC) | until 24 Nov 2026 | EU 0% (EBA) | **Single transformation** — imported fabric OK |
| EBA transition | ~2026 → ~2029 | EU 0% continues | Single transformation continues |
| After transition | ~2029 onward | 0% **only if GSP+** granted; else standard GSP (0–4.5% on knits) or 12% MFN | ⚠️ Standard GSP/GSP+ apparel typically requires **"double transformation" (made from yarn)** — imported *fabric* may no longer confer origin |

**Verdict:** Your import-fabric, cut-&-sew model is **duty-free into the EU with certainty
through roughly 2029.** After that it depends on Nepal securing GSP+ *and* on whether the
stricter double-transformation rule applies — which could break the "Chinese fabric" model.
**Therefore: build export relationships in the next 2–3 years while the advantage is
guaranteed.** Frame it to buyers honestly as "lock in duty-free sourcing now."

⚠️ Confirm the exact post-2029 rule-of-origin treatment with TEPC before making long-term
promises to a buyer — this is the item most likely to change.

## PART 3 — Buyer-required compliance & certifications

This is your **#1 conversion gap.** Domestic resellers never asked; serious EU/UK brands
require it before placing an order.

| Cert | Who wants it | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| **amfori BSCI** or **Sedex/SMETA** (social/ethical audit) | Nearly all EU/UK brands & sourcing agents | **Do first** — highest ROI on conversion |
| **Oeko-Tex Standard 100** (product safety / no harmful substances) | Many brands, esp. for skin-contact knitwear | Second |
| **WRAP** | Some US buyers | Later / on request |
| **ISO 9001** (quality mgmt) | Nice-to-have credibility | Optional |

**How to get audited:** engage an accredited audit body operating in Nepal (SGS, Bureau
Veritas, Intertek, TÜV all operate in South Asia). They inspect labor practices, wages,
hours, safety, building, environmental handling. Expect a preparation period + audit day +
corrective actions. Budget several thousand USD and 1–3 months.

**Until audited, present honestly:** "compliance-ready; BSCI/Sedex audit booked for [date]."
Never claim a certificate you don't hold — buyers verify, and a false claim ends the
relationship. "Audit booked" converts far better than "none."

## PART 4 — How an export order actually flows + logistics

**Order-to-cash sequence** (this is your existing "consult → sample → bulk → deliver" flow
with export paperwork added at the end):

```
1 Inquiry   → send line-card + prices
2 Sample    → make (7–14 days); buyer pays courier; buyer approves
3 Agree     → Proforma Invoice: qty, price, Incoterm, lead time
4 Payment   → T/T deposit (e.g. 30%) OR L/C for big orders
5 Materials → order Chinese fabric/trims against the confirmed order
6 Produce   → cut → sew → finish (bulk 30–45 days) → in-line + final QC
7 Docs      → prepare export document set + get Certificate of Origin
8 Customs   → file export declaration (pragyapan patra) with EXIM code
9 Ship      → truck to Kolkata port (sea) OR air from Kathmandu
10 Balance  → buyer pays balance per terms → repeat
```

**Document set per shipment** (a customs clearing agent / freight forwarder prepares these —
hire one for your first shipments, don't DIY):
- Commercial Invoice (with the **Statement on Origin** text if < €6,000 and no REX yet)
- Packing List
- **Certificate of Origin** (from the Chamber)
- Export customs declaration (pragyapan patra) — filed with EXIM code
- Bill of Lading (sea) or Air Waybill (air)
- Any buyer-required inspection / test certificates

**Incoterms for a landlocked exporter** — quote one of:
- **EXW Gaidakot** (buyer handles everything from your gate — simplest for you)
- **FOB Kolkata** (you get it to the ship at Kolkata — more attractive to buyers; you bear
  Nepal→Kolkata inland cost). Recommended once you're comfortable.

**Logistics reality:** Nepal is landlocked. Bulk goes by **road to Kolkata port** (adds cost
+ ~1–2 weeks vs. a coastal factory); **air freight from Kathmandu** is faster but far pricier
(use for samples and urgent small orders only). Bake the inland/freight cost into your price
and set honest lead times — this is a permanent structural handicap, so don't hide it, price
it.

## PART 5 — Finding & winning export buyers

Aim **everything** at the niche: **small private-label brands, DTC/e-commerce labels,
promo/merch companies, and the sourcing agents who serve them** — buyers who need 300–2,000
pcs/style, custom design, top quality, and duty-free landed cost. NOT commodity bulk buyers
(you lose those to Bangladesh).

**Channels, run in parallel:**
1. **Your website + SEO** — "Nepal t-shirt manufacturer", "duty-free knitwear Nepal", "LDC garment sourcing" (already building)
2. **B2B marketplaces** — profiles on **Alibaba** and **Foursource** with the same story + link back to your site
3. **Sourcing agents / Kathmandu buying houses** — they place foreign brands' orders locally; warm, high-conversion
4. **Direct outreach** to small private-label brands (your Khangri Sourcing email is this play)
5. **TEPC / GAN buyer directories + trade fairs** — once you're a member
6. **LinkedIn** — company page + founder outreach

**What buyers ask for first (have these ready):** line-card/capability PDF, price per piece,
MOQ, lead time, and compliance status. The **one-page capability PDF is the single
most-requested asset** — build it.

**Realistic expectations:** B2B apparel sourcing is slow. Expect months from first contact
to first order, lots of no-replies, sample rounds before commitment. One or two solid
private-label relationships that reorder is a real win — not dozens of buyers.

## PART 6 — Market & tariff data (2026)

| Market | Your duty | The reality | Play |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EU (27)** | **0%** (EBA) → transition to ~2029 → then GSP+/GSP | MFN rate is **12%** on cotton knit tees (HS 6109) — that's your headroom vs non-preferential origins. **BUT Bangladesh, Cambodia, Myanmar also get 0%** and Bangladesh pays lower wages (~$113/mo vs Nepal ~$141/mo) | Hero market. Duty-free is table-stakes among LDCs — win on quality + low MOQ + full-package |
| **UK** | **0%** (DCTS Comprehensive) → Enhanced Preferences ~2029 | ⭐ MORE durable than EU: since **1 Jan 2026**, DCTS needs only **cut & sew in Nepal** (single transformation) — imported fabric qualifies — and this apparel rule is **preserved when Nepal moves to Enhanced Preferences (~2029)**. Duty-free through at least 2029. | **Prioritise for long-term deals** — the fabric-import model survives graduation here, unlike the EU. Verify exact 6109 duty under Enhanced before multi-year contracts. |
| **USA** | MFN (~13–15% on knits) **+ 10% reciprocal** | Nepal's +10% is the **lowest add-on in South Asia** — India far higher. Relative advantage vs India/China, but policy-dependent (can change) | Ride the "cheaper tariff than India" moment while it lasts; buyers diversifying away from Asia |

**Key takeaway:** the duty-free story is real but **shared**. It gets a buyer to look; your
**top-notch quality, flexible MOQ, own-design/CAD, and full-package service** are what close
and retain them. Never position as "low cost" — that invites the Bangladesh comparison you
lose.
